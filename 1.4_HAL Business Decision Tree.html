💡 Threshold
Below the threshold, splitting across vendors is not meaningful — one vendor can take the full route.
💡 Cap ratio
Linearly decaying cap — as route volume grows, max vendor share shrinks, limiting concentration risk. Hard floor at 47%.
💡 Why 70%
The 70% baseline ensures the majority of shipment volume is prioritised to more reliable, pre-approved Preferred vendors.
💡 Why skip Mode B after Mode A?
Mode A exhausts budget headroom by shifting volume to cheaper Normal vendors. Once complete, the weighted cost sits at or near the budget ceiling — leaving no room for Mode B, which moves volume to faster but typically more expensive Preferred vendors. Any Mode B move would immediately breach the budget.

Decision 2 — Capacity Cap (Detail)

flowchart TD A([For each vendor on the route]) --> R{Which region\nis this bid?} R -->|SEA| B1{Weekly volume\n≥ 14,000 kg?} R -->|LATAM| B2{Weekly volume\n≥ 22,400 kg?} B1 -->|NO| C[Effective Cap = Stated Capacity\nNo cap applied] B2 -->|NO| C B1 -->|YES| D["Cap Ratio = max( 0.84 − 0.000003 × Volume, 0.47 ) Vendor Cap = floor( Volume × Cap Ratio ) Effective Cap = min( Stated Capacity, Vendor Cap )"] B2 -->|YES| D C --> E([Effective cap confirmed]) D --> E

Why the volume threshold?

Threshold = Min daily volume × 7 days × 2 vendors.
The cap activates only when the route generates enough weekly volume to sustain at least 2 vendors at their minimum viable daily volume. Below the threshold, splitting across vendors is not meaningful — one vendor can take the full route. Above it, there is sufficient scale to enforce a cap and keep multiple vendors viable.
RegionMin Daily VolumeCalculationThreshold
SEA1,000 kg/day1,000 × 7 × 214,000 kg/week
LATAM1,600 kg/day1,600 × 7 × 222,400 kg/week

Why the cap ratio formula?

Linearly decaying concentration cap — as route volume grows, the maximum share any single vendor can hold shrinks, limiting concentration risk. The hard floor at 47% (above 123,333 kg/week) is a non-negotiable policy limit. The formula was derived from concentration risk analysis: policy targets were set at key volume levels, a linear curve was fitted to those points, yielding the 0.84 intercept and 0.000003 slope, with a 47% floor applied as an absolute minimum.

Decision 3 — Bucket Structure (Detail)

Main Workflow — Mixed Route (Preferred + Normal vendors)

flowchart LR A([Route has both\nPreferred and Normal vendors]) --> E["Quota target = min( 70% × Volume , Σ Preferred eff. cap ) Shared = Volume − Quota filled If Preferred cap < 70% target → Quota shrinks to Preferred cap → Shared absorbs the difference"] E --> F([Proceed to fill])
Why 70%? The 70% baseline ensures the majority of shipment volume is prioritised to more reliable, pre-approved Preferred vendors — supporting service stability and business relationship commitments.
Note: The 70% is the starting baseline, not a hard floor. Mode A optimisation can reduce Preferred share below 70% if needed to meet the budget target.

Edge Cases — non-standard route compositions

Preferred Vendors Only

Quota = min(100% × Volume, Σ Preferred eff. cap)
Shared bucket does not exist.
If Preferred cap < volume → shortfall is permanently unallocated.

Normal Vendors Only

All volume goes to Normal vendors.
Quota bucket does not exist.
Fill by: Cost ↑ then LT ↑ then file order.

Decision 6 — Optimisation (Detail)

Mode A — Budget Reduction

flowchart TD A([Enter Mode A]) --> B{Cost > budget?} B -->|No| SKIP([Skip — cost already within budget]) B -->|Yes| C["Find all valid moves on every route: Source = Preferred vendor with Quota kg Dest = Normal vendor on same route Valid only if: cost of Dest < cost of Source"] C --> D{Any valid\nmoves exist?} D -->|No| STUCK([Mode A ends\nBudget may still be over target]) D -->|Yes| E[Pick move with\nhighest cost saving per 0.01kg] E --> F[Apply: −1 kg Source Quota\n+1 kg Dest Shared\nRecalculate global cost] F --> G{Cost ≤ budget?} G -->|No| C G -->|Yes| DONE([Mode A complete\nProceed to Final Allocation])
💡 Why Mode A does not proceed to Mode B
Mode A exhausts available budget headroom by shifting volume from Preferred vendors to cheaper Normal vendors. Once Mode A completes, the weighted cost sits at or very near the budget ceiling. Mode B moves volume between Preferred vendors toward faster but typically more expensive options — any such move would immediately push cost over budget. With no budget slack remaining, Mode B has no valid moves, so the system goes directly to Final Allocation.

Mode B — LT Improvement

flowchart TD A([Enter Mode B]) --> B{Preferred LT\n> LT target?} B -->|No| DONE([Target met ✅]) B -->|Yes| C["Find all valid moves on every route: Source = slower Preferred vendor with Quota kg Dest = faster Preferred vendor on same route Valid only if: resulting global cost ≤ budget"] C --> D{Any valid\nmoves exist?} D -->|No| STUCK([Mode B ends\nLT target may not be reached]) D -->|Yes| E["Prefer: zero or negative cost moves first Then: best LT gain per unit of cost"] E --> F[Apply: −1 kg Source Quota\n+1 kg Dest Quota\nRecalculate LT and cost] F --> B
Mode B is only reached when Mode A was skipped (i.e. baseline cost was already within budget). If Mode A ran, the system proceeds directly to Final Allocation.
One-way switch: If Mode A runs, the system proceeds directly to Final Allocation — Mode B is not attempted. Mode B only runs when the baseline cost is already within budget and no Mode A pass was needed.

Why was the LT target of 2.5 days not reached?

Mode B improves LT by moving kg from Vendor B (slow) to Vendor A (fast). Vendor A's effective capacity is 8,000 kg — it was full after 2,000 moves. To reach 2.5 days, Vendor A would need approximately 9,333 kg, which exceeds its 8,000 kg capacity. The LT target is physically unachievable with the current vendor capacities — the best possible result on this route is 3.29 days.
